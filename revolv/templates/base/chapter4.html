    Collecting Information
    First step is collecting all the information
    we’ll need to size up a solar project

    Once you’ve picked a nonprofit you want to work with, and you’ve had an initial discussion with them about the benefits of going solar with RE-volv, you’ll need to collect a few pieces of information from them. Namely, the condition of their roof, their electric bills, photos of their electrical boxes and their financial statements.
    A. Roof Condition
    The first and most important thing we need to figure out is, is this a good roof for solar? With the address of the nonprofit, we’ll be able to use satellite imagery to see if the roof has a good orientation for solar and isn’t shaded. But we also need to know how old the roof is and what condition it’s in. The reason for this is, if it’s an old roof and it’s not in the best condition, it’s not a good time to put solar on it. If the nonprofit has to replace the roof soon then they’ll have to take the panels off and put them back on again and that adds extra costs. It’s best to find a nonprofit who has replaced their roof sometime in the last 5 years preferably. (If it’s under 10 years old and is in good condition and has a long expected roof life that could work too.) Also if the roof is very old and the nonprofit has plans to replace it soon anyway, the nonprofit could combine the roof replacement and the solar installation which has some great advantages, or we could plan to install the solar equipment after the new roof is finished. In order to figure out the roof condition, you’ll have to speak with someone at the nonprofit who knows that information.
    B. Electric bills for the last 12 months
    Before we build a solar system, we need to figure out how much electricity your nonprofit consumes each year. That way we can put the right amount of solar panels on the roof. The best way to capture this information is to get the electric bills for the last 12 months. There are a few ways you can do this:
    1. Utility API- If you’re in California or in select states on the East Coast or Midwest you can use a program called Utility API. Utility API essentially lets the customer give RE-volv authorization to access the electric bill data from their utility. It’s the easiest way to do it, if the nonprofit you’re working with is in one of the service territories they serve. Find out if your nonprofits utility can use Utility API at utilityapi.com.
    2. For those of you not in Utility API territory, many of the utilities are increasingly providing bills online. If the nonprofit has online billing then they can easily download the last 12 months electric bill data.
    3. If they don’t have access to an online billing portal, some utilities will still be able to send you the data electronically if you ask for it. The best thing to do is call the customer care line of the utility and ask if there is a way to have the electric bill data sent electronically. Typically this requires the nonprofit to sign an authorization form. Or if the nonprofit asks the utility they might be able to provide that without much hassle.
    4. Lastly, if there’s no easy way to get it electronically then the best thing to do is go over to the nonprofit one day and ask them if you can photocopy the last 12 months of their electric bills.
    C. Take photos of electrical boxes
    For Panel Box photos, please take the following shots:
    1. A picture of the Main Panel's Main Breaker Switch
    2. A wide shot with all of the breakers in the box and any empty spaces shown.
    3. A picture of the Panel's label.
    4. A shot from at least 10 feet away to show anything around the box.
